What is the 'Male Gaze'?

Male Gaze

/meɪl geɪz/ noun

a way of viewing women from a heterosexual male perspective as objects of pleasure or desire.

"Men act and women appear. Men look at women. Women watch themselves being looked at"

- John Berger

Case Study: Harley Quinn in Suicide Squad vs. Birds of Prey

Harley Quinn is a staple villian in the DC Comic universe. She is a playful character that is known for her zany clown-themed costumes and association with Batman's archnemesis: the Joker.

She has been featured in two movies in the DC expanded universe: Suicide Squad, which was directed by a man, and Birds of Prey, which was directed by a woman.

Let's investigate the difference in her portrayal, as played by the same actress, Margot Robbie.

In her Suicide Squad appearance, Harley is shown:

-- with a "strategically ripped" t-shirt to show more of her skin

-- not wearing any pants, with all of her legs showing

-- looking seductively and walking towards the camera/viewer

-- wearing a padded bra and an actual dog collar

In her Birds of Prey appearance, Harley is shown:

-- with shorter pigtails, unable to be pulled on

-- in characteristically eccentric clothing, but more practical

-- smiling in a way that seems genuine, not a forced 'sexy' smirk

-- holding a comic-book accurate hammer, rather than a phallic bat

Harley Quinn is an inherently sexy character-- however, her role in Suicide Squad was reduced to only being a sexual object. In Birds of Prey, Harley's femininity is still emphasized, but as an aspect of her character, not her entire character.

How does this translate onto the Internet?

'Selfies' have become a primary method of showcasing yourself online. They, too, perpetrate the male gaze.

This picture shows the commonly accepted "best" angle for women to take selfies.

At first glance, this may seem inconsequential-- however, the camera is actually taking a position of authority.

The camera is above the woman, looking down at her. This makes her a smaller, diminutive entity.

If you think about the camera as a type of gaze-- as it creates the way that the audience of the photo will view the subject-- It is enforcing the type of misogyny perpetrated by the aforementioend male gaze.

On the other hand, men primarily take selfies straight-on or from below

If we apply the same logic as above, men are depicting themselves neutrally, or even in a position of power: taking the image from below makes them appear larger.

This culture is so ingrained in society that we do not even think about it, but it does create and reinforce patriarchal power structures.

Posting selfies is a ubiquitous aspect of internet culture and social meda. The camera is a form of gaze, and the way it portrays people-- even if the angle is self-selected-- can emphasize the oppression of women and unequal social power held by men.
